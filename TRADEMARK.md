This policy protects the **TSP integrity domain** — the assurance that a TSP mark
means a verifiable, conformant, LexiCo-authorized thing. It does **not** restrict
public verification, which is free.

## Permitted without permission (nominative / referential use)

- Stating, truthfully, that your software **verifies** TSP TrustEnvelopes or
  implements the **public TSP verification** path.
- Referring to "the Trust Standard Protocol" or "TSP" to describe the protocol,
  in prose, documentation, or academic work.
- Linking to the specification and official conformance suite.

These uses must not imply LexiCo endorsement, certification, or authorization
where none exists.

## Requires LexiCo authorization

- Describing any product or service as **"TSP-compatible"** or **"TSP-certified."**
- Using the **TrustBadge** visual marks, or any confusingly similar badge.
- Claiming **participation in the official TSP integrity domain**, or that you are
  an authorized **issuer** or **certifier**.
- Using the covered marks in a product name, company name, domain, or logo.

Authorization requires passing the **current official verifier / conformance
suite** and, where issuance or certification is involved, a **LexiCo-authorized
TSP license**. Historical alpha-preview materials confer none of this.

## Prohibited

- **Counterfeiting**: forging TrustEnvelopes, fabricating TrustBadges, or making
  false TSP-compatible / TSP-certified claims.
- Weakening or removing the fail-closed license gate (e.g. the
  `402 unlicensed_platform` path) or canonicalization in a build presented as TSP.
